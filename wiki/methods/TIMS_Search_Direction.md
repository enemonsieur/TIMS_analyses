# TIMS Search Direction

This page stores the TIMS-specific context that must be used together with [[Literature_Search_Framework]] before running paper searches.

## What It Measures

The goal is to find a neural or behavioral marker/model that can be tested in one person or repeated sessions to decide whether TIMS produces an interpretable effect.

The search should not look for papers that are merely about TMS, tACS, tCS, oscillations, TEPs, cognition, or biomarkers. It should look for papers where the method, marker, result, limitation, and author interpretation create a usable opening for a TIMS experiment.

## Where It Helped

Use this page whenever selecting, searching, or reviewing papers for TIMS search-angle design.

The companion rule is: use the general framework first, then this TIMS direction page to filter what counts as relevant.

## Known Failure Modes

Current TIMS advantages:

- TIMS may provide stronger rhythmic perturbation than weak-field tACS/tCS.
- TIMS may be less pulse-like and less disruptive than classical TMS.
- TIMS may support repeated, network-level, dynamic, or rhythm-dependent stimulation.

Current TIMS constraints:

- No large sample should be assumed; papers should support N=1, repeated-session, dose-response, or strong within-subject logic.
- MEP-only validation is not enough; MEP papers are useful only if they reveal a transferable neural reactivity or excitability marker.
- Artifact and recovery limits matter; papers requiring clean online EEG during stimulation are not direct fits unless they explain a transferable post-stimulation or artifact-resistant readout.
- Deep-target-only paradigms are excluded as direct targets. They can be background only if they explain a mechanism transferable to accessible cortex or measurable behavior.
- Papers must support accessible neural or behavioral readouts.

Reject patterns:

- A paper is about a relevant marker but does not show how that marker could answer the TIMS proof question.
- A TMS confound paper only explains sensory/artifact cleanup and does not identify a usable neural reactivity marker.
- A rhythmic stimulation paper reports broadband or generic neural change without a specificity contrast.
- A paper only has an abstract available; mark as needs full text rather than summarizing.
- A paper depends on large-N behavior, deep inaccessible targets, or MEP amplitude alone.

## TIMS Verdict

Current accepted angle 1: **under-mapped response landscapes**.

Use this angle to find papers where rhythmic stimulation, sensory drive, or oscillatory perturbation manipulates frequency, phase, dose, timing, task state, baseline state, or individual resonance, producing a neural marker whose response landscape is not fully mapped. The useful paper pattern is not "the marker is stable"; it is "the marker depends on controllable parameters that could be mapped within one subject."

Current accepted angle 2: **useful neural reactivity markers where TMS interpretation is limited by confounds**.

Use this angle to find papers where TMS or pulse-like perturbation produces a neural marker that could index cortical sensitivity, excitability, reactivity, or network response, but interpretation is limited by auditory, somatosensory, motor, timing, artifact, or suprathreshold confounds. The useful paper pattern is not "sensory confounds exist"; it is "a potentially useful neural marker is hard to interpret because of those confounds."

## Open Questions

- Which accessible marker family should be prioritized first: TEP/ERP, steady-state response, EEG power, connectivity, phase-locking, behavior paired with EEG, or MEP-plus-EEG reactivity?
- How much online artifact can be tolerated before a paper becomes background only?
- Which response landscapes are practical to map in one person: frequency, phase, dose, timing, task state, baseline state, or individual resonance?
